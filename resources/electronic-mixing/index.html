Electronic Music Mixing Guide

Master the Art of Electronic Music Mixing

Electronic music presents unique challenges in mixing. From taming aggressive synths to creating space in a dense mix, understanding these key techniques will help you achieve professional results.

The difference between an amateur and professional electronic mix often comes down to these essential techniques.

1. Master Sidechaining

Sidechaining is essential in electronic music, particularly for creating that signature "pumping" effect and ensuring your kick cuts through the mix.

Key Applications

  • Kick/Bass Relationship: Use sidechain compression to duck the bass when the kick hits
  • Creating Space: Sidechain pads and atmospheric elements to the kick for clarity
Pro Tip: Use a transparent sidechain plugin (like Kickstart or Xfer Records LFOTool) to control the shape of the "pump" without affecting the character of the sound.

2. Layer Your Bass for Depth and Power

Bass is the foundation of electronic music. A powerful low-end can make or break a track, but layering bass sounds is key to achieving a thick, punchy, and clean low end.

The Mistake

  • Relying on a single bass sound or not layering bass properly, resulting in a weak, undefined low end.

How to Avoid It

  • Layer Different Types of Bass: Combine sub-bass (below 80Hz) with mid-bass for definition.
  • EQ Each Layer: Give each bass layer its own frequency range to prevent overlap.
Pro Tip: Use saturation on your bass layers to add warmth and harmonics, making them stand out without adding extra volume.

3. Create Width with Stereo Imaging

Stereo width is crucial in electronic music to give the track space, movement, and depth. A track that sounds too narrow can feel confined.

How to Create Width

  • Stereo Widening: Use stereo widening effects on synths, percussion, and pads.
  • Mid/Side EQ: Treat the mid and side elements separately for clarity.
  • Delay and Reverb: Create depth with different reverbs for different elements.
Pro Tip: Keep the low end and vocals centered in the mix—these elements tend to get lost if they are spread out too wide.

4. Use High-Pass Filtering Wisely

The Mistake

  • Not using high-pass filters to clean up unnecessary low-end content, especially on non-bass instruments.

How to Avoid It

  • High-Pass Filters on Non-Essential Tracks: Apply to pads, vocals, and percussion.
  • Clean the Low-Mids: Use gentle dips around 200-400Hz to reduce muddiness.
Pro Tip: Use high-pass filters creatively on synths, effects, and vocals, but always listen in the context of the full mix.

5. Don't Forget About Arrangement

While mixing is essential, arrangement is key to making sure your electronic track doesn't become over-crowded.

Key Techniques

  • Use Automation: Bring elements in and out dynamically.
  • Create Movement: Incorporate subtle changes in texture and sound design.
  • Use Silence: Create impact with strategic breaks and drops.
Pro Tip: Focus on less is more for parts where the vocal or lead synth is present. Sometimes, subtle background elements allow the primary elements to shine.
